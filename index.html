Innate Immunity

  • its the natural immune response of the body that is ready to recognize and eliminate pathogens and dead cells

  • innate response mechanisms are inherited

  • its components are:

    • Cytokines

    • Physical and chemical barriers

    • Phagocytic cells and Natural killer cells

    • Blood proteins

  • The first line of defense is given by the epithelial barrier of the skin and mucous barrier

  • There are mechanical actions that remove pathogens like tears flushing microbes away from the eye and urine washing microbes out of the urinary tract

  • There are chemical narriers including the hCLK acid of the stomach and lysozymes

  • if microbes somehow bypass all the barriers, they are attacked by:

    • Phagocytes

    • Special lymphocytes called innate lymphoid cells which include Natural killer cells (NKs)

    • Plasma proteins

  • innate immunity also initiates the repair of damaged tissue

  • It also initiates the adaptive immune system


    General features of innate immunity

  • it eliminates microbes through 2 mechanisms:

    • 1- Acute-inflammatory response

      • Leukocytes and plasma proteins accumulate at the site of infection or tissue damage

      • these cells and proteins work together to kill foreign microbes and eliminate damaged tissue

      • the pathogens are killed through phagocytosis

    • 2-Antiviral defense:

      • Natural killer cells kill virus infected cells

      • cytokines and type-1 interferons block viral replication in host cells

  • No or very little memory cells are made

  • phagocytes recognize structures that are shared by various classes of microbes like:

    • Bacterial LPS found on the outer membrane of bacteria

    • terminal mannose residues on glycoproteins, recognising them helps in identifying potential pathogens

    • dsRNA in viruses

    • unmethylated CpG nucleotides which are common in bacterial DNA

  • The receptors of the innate immune response that recognise these shared structures are called Pattern recognition receptors (PRR)

  • The shared structures found on microbes that the immune response detects are called

    Pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs). these structures are essential for the survival of the microbe and essential for our immune system to be able to detect the microbes

  • The Innate immune system (IIS) recognises molecules released from damaged or dead cells, these molecules are called damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs)

  • the response to DAMPs is to eliminate the damaged cells and initiate tissue repair

  • Innate responses occur even following sterile injuries (injuries with no pathogens involved) such as amputations

  • The receptors of the IIS are encoded by inherited genes and are identical in all cells, so identical receptors are found on all of the cells of a specific type

    The receptors of the IIS

  • Receptors of IIS can be found on the cell surface of phagocytes where they detct extracellular microbes

  • In vesicles

  • In the cytosol where they sense any cytoplasmic microbes

    Major families of Receptors in the Innate Immune system

  • Toll-Like Receptors (TLR)

    • Different TLRs are specific to different components of microbes

      • TLRs recognizes glycolipids and dsRNA

      • TLR 3, 7, 8 are specific for viral ssRNA and dsRNA

      • TLR-4 is specific for bacterial LPS

      • TLR-5 is specific for bacterial flagellin

      • TLR-9 is specific for unmethylated CpG DNA which is present in high amounts in bacteria

    • When TLRs detect pathogens they send signals that activate 2 types of transcription factors

      • 1- Nuclear factor kB: release cytokines that promote inflammation and activate endothelial adhesion molecules that stick immune cells to the infected area which increases the efficiency of the Immune response

      • 2- Interferon regulatory factors: stimulate production of antiviral cytokines and type 1 interferons

  • NOD-like receptors (NLRs)

    • NLRs are a large family of cytosolic receptors that sense DAMPs and PAMPs in the cytoplasm and initiate inflammation

    • 3 important ones, NOD-1 and NOD-2 are specific to bacterial peptidoglycan. they generate signals that activate NF-kB

  • RIG-Like receptors

    • they detect viral RNA produced by viruses in the cytosol

    • they stimulate the production of anti-viral type 1 interferon (IFN)

  • Cytosolic DNA Sensors (CDSs)

    • recognise cytosolic microbial dsDNA

    • initiate autophagy (cell death) and IFN production

  • Lectin receptors

    • they are specific to fungal glycans and terminal mannose residues; lectin receptors are called mannose receptors

    • they are involved in the phagocytosis of fungi and bacteria

      Components of the innate Immune System

  • One of the components of the IIS is soluble proteins: (these proteins enhance phagocytosis)

    • 1- C-reactive protein (CRP): Carries out a process called opsonization which is coating he microbe with proteins and lipids that make it easier for the phagocyte to recognise it

    • 2- Plasma MBL:

      • Recognise microbial carbohydrates and coats microbes for phagocytosis, they also activate the complement cascade by the lectin pathway

    • 2- Cytokines: they are responsible for communications between leukocytes and other cells

      The Skin

  • its the first line of defence against pathogens

  • made up of epidermis (most outer layer), dermis, hypodermis (most inner layer)

  • the epidermis is slightly acidic so it inhibits bacterial growth

  • the dermis contains sebaceous glands which secrete sebum, sebum inhibits the growth of bacteria and fungi

  • Keratin on the surface of the skin provides resistance against bacterial enzymes and makes the skin waterproof which reduces bacterial growth.

  • fatty acids on the skins surface create a salty, dry, acidic environment that inhibit bacterial growth Epidermis

  • Keratinocytes produce cytokines important for inflammation

  • Langerhan cells which are immature dendritic cells capture antigens and migrate to lymph nodes

  • Intraepidermal lymphocytes have specific receptors on their surface that allow them to recognise specific antigens on pathogens

    Dermis:

  • Contains T lymphocytes (T cells) and scattered macrophages

  • T cells are either Activated normal cells or memory T cells that remember past encounters with antigens Epithelial Tissue

  • They line the surface and cavities of our body and play an important role in protecting us from infections

  • They form a physical barrier that keeps pathogens away

  • Produce antimicrobial substances called defensins and cathelicidins

  • They contain intraepithelial T lymphocytes which detect any pathogen trying to breach the epithelial barrier and kill them. They detect pathogens by recognizing lipids found on the surface of pathogens

    Mucous membrane

  • It lines the nose, mouth, lungs, digestive and urinary tract

  • It is a non-specific barrier

  • Its made up of a layer of epithelial cells bound by tight junctions

  • The mucosal surface of the Gastrointestinal tract and respiratory tract are common entry doors for pathogens

  • The mucosal immune system produces a high level of a specific antibody called immunoglobulin A (IgA). IgA is present in bodily fluids like tears and saliva

  • Lymph nodes and tonsils located in the oral and nasal area play a part in the mucosal immune system, they monitor microbes entering through those regions and initiate immune responses to eliminate the intruders

    The chemistry of the mucus immune system

  • Mucus secreted by the respiratory and GI tract traps pathogens

  • Pathogens can be expelled mechanically through the flushing action of mucus and saliva, or by coughing and sneezing in the respiratory tract

  • Saliva and tears contain enzymes such as lysozymes that destroy bacterial cell walls

  • Vaginal secretions become acidic following the first period of menstruation

  • Mothers milk contains a powerful enzyme called lactoperoxidase

  • Few pathogens are able to survive the low pH and destructive enzymes found in the gastric acid Normal Flora

  • They are the microbes(mostly bacteria) that live in and on the body and are beneficial to us

  • They provide protection by competing with pathogens and producing antibiotic substances called bacteriocins that kill other bacteria

  • Normal flora produces: (all of these substances work to destroy other bacteria)

    • Bacteriocidins

    • Defensins

    • Cationic proteins

    • Lactoferrin

  • Anaerobic bacteria produce toxic metabolic products and free fatty acids that inhibit other organisms

  • Lactobacilli found in the female genital tract produces lactic acid which lowers the pH

    Fever

  • The normal body temperature is between 36.5 to 37.5

  • Pyrogens are molecules that stimulate the hypothalamus to increase the body’s temperature

  • There are 2 types of Pyrogens:

  • 1- Exogenous pyrogens: Pyrogens that come from outside the body

    • Bacterial toxins

    • The cytoplasm of lysed bacteria

    • Antigen and antibody complexes

  • 2- Endogenous pyrogens: Pyrogens made by the body

    • Interleukin-1 made by phagocytes

    • Tumor necrosis factor made by phagocytes How does fever help in the innate immune system?

  • It increases the mobility of leukocytes, strengthens and accelerates them

  • Reduces free plasma iron, which is necessary for pathogens to divide and produce toxins

  • Triggers the production of endogenous pyrogen

  • Leads to the division and activation of Lymphocytes (T and B cells)

  • It inactivates Heat sensitive pathogens

    Cells of Innate immunity

    1- Phagocytes: Neutrophils and Monocytes/Macrophages

    - Neutrophils and monocytes are blood cells that recognise and ingest pathogens


    A- Neutrophils: (Also called polymorphonuclear leukocytes - PMN)

  • They are the first cell type to respond to most infections, especially bacterial and fungal

  • They are the main part in inflammation, they quickly gather at the site of an infection

  • They are found in the blood but can quickly migrate to extravascular tissue dealing with an infection

  • Neutrophils are recruited to sites of tissue damage where they initiate the removal of cell debris. This occurs only in the absence of infections

  • They do not provide a prolonged defence

  • They contain microbicidal granules which they release into the extracellular environment

  • The granule content traps bacteria and fungi and also disarms pathogens with antimicrobial proteins such as neutrophil elastase

  • They serve as a physical barrier that prevents the further spread of pathogens B- Monocytes/Macrophages:

  • They also carry out the same function as neutrophils

  • During infections, monocytes enter the blood and differentiate into cells called macrophages

  • Macrophages can survive for longer periods of time at the infection site compared to neutrophils

  • Monocytes and macrophages are 2 stages of the same cell type, this is often called the mononuclear phagocyte system

  • Role of Macrophages in the Innate Immune system

    • They ingest and destroy microbes

    • Produce cytokines that induce inflammation

    • Clear dead tissue

    • They are important in both the cellular and humoral adaptive immune system

    • They act as Antigen-presenting cells (APCs), they present antigens of pathogens theyve destroyed on their surface and this allows other immune cells to recognize the antigen and produce specific antibodies

    • They may kill infected cells or tumour cells through the release of enzymes

  • Activation of Macrophages occurs through 2 different pathways

  • 1- Classical Macrophage activation

    • Initiated by signals from Toll-Like receptors(TLR) and the cytokine IFN (antiviral type 1 interferon)

    • Macrophages activated through this pathway are called M1 and are involved in destroying bacteria and triggering inflammation

  • 2- Alternative Macrophage Activation

    • Occurs in the absence of TLR signals and is induced by the cytokines IL-4 and IL-13

    • Macrophages activated through this pathway are called M2 and are involved in tissue repair and terminating inflammation

      C- Mast Cells

  • Mast cells can be activated by either TLRs when they detect pathogens or by special antibodies

  • Mast cells contain granules that contain:

    • Vasoactive amines like histamine that cause vasodilation and increased capillary permeability

    • Proteolytic enzymes that can kill bacteria

  • Mast cells secrete lipid mediators like prostaglandins and release cytokines. These molecules stimulate inflammation

  • They protect against helminths and other pathogens

  • Theyre responsible for symptoms of allergic diseases. During allergic reactions, mast cells release their contents when they shouldnt

    D- Dendritic cells

  • They capture pathogenic proteins and display them on their surface for T cells to initiate the adaptive immune response

  • They respond to pathogens through producing many cytokines that have multiple 2 functions:

    • Initiate inflammation

    • Stimulate the adaptive immune system

  • Theyre the bridge between the innate immune system and the adaptive system

  • Theyre the most efficient APCs and theyre the first type of cell to present pathogenic proteins to activate T cells

    E- Innate Lymphoid Cells (ILCs)

  • ILCs are lymphocyte like cells that produce cytokines similar to those secreted by T helper lymphocytes

  • They provide early defense against infections

  • Group 1 ILCs and natural killer cells share some similarities and are related F- Natural Killer Cells (NKs)

  • NKs recognise infected and stressed cells and kill them

  • NK cells Detect Interleukin-12 produced by macrophages and release Macrophage-activating cytokines (IFN-γ) that increase the effectiveness of macrophages at phagocytosis

  • They are non-specific

  • They contain granules in their cytoplasm

  • When they detect an infected cells, they empty their granules to the extracellular space

  • The granule proteins enter the infected cells and activate enzymes that induce apoptosis

  • NKs eliminate cellular reservoirs of infection caused by intracellular pathogens such as viruses

  • The cytotoxic behavior of NK cells is similar to that of Cytotoxic T cells


    Lymphocytes with limited diversity

  • NK-T cells recognise pathogenic lipids bound to class 1 MHC- related molecule called CD1

  • B-1 Cells, theyre found in the peritoneal cavity and mucosal tissues and they produce antibodies, They produce most of the Circulating IgM antibodies found in the blood

  • Marginal-zone B cells are present in the spleen, when they detect polysachharide-rich pathogens found in the blood they quickly release antibodies

  • All these cells respond in ways considered part of the adaptive immune response but they have features of the innate immune response, (They have a rapid response and can detect a limited number of antigens, so theyre not very specific)

    Cytokines

  • Dendritic cells, macrophages, mast cells and others release cytokines that mediate many aspects of the innate immune system

  • 1- Tumor necrosis factor, Interleukin-1 (IL-1) and chemokines are the main cytokines involved in calling blood neutrophils and monocytes towards the site of the infection

  • TNF and IL-1 also induce fever

  • TNF, IL-1 and IL-6 stimulate the liver to produce C-reactive protein and fibrinogen

  • IFN-y is a macrophage-activating Cytokine

  • IL-15 is important for the development and maturation of NK cells

  • Type 1 interferons and IL-12 enhance the killing function of NK cells

Innate Immune reactions

-The Innate immune reactions responds to pathogens in 3 different ways


  1. Acute Inflammatory response

    • Inflammation is the reaction that brings neutrophils and monocytes to the site of the injection

    • Its the first response of the innate immune system to infections

    • Firstly neutrophils are recruited to the infection site

    • Neutrophils trigger the immune system by releasing factors that summon other innate immune cells and lymphocytes

    • Inflammation establishes a physical barrier against the spread of infection

    • It promotes healing of any damaged tissue after the infection is cleared How does is inflammation triggered?

    • Cytokines released by macrophages, dendritic cells, mast cells and others increase the permeability of blood vessels.

    • This leads to the entry of plasma proteins into the infected tissue.

    • This promotes the movement of leukocytes from the blood into the infected tissue as well

    • Leukocytes Destroy microbes and clear damaged cells and lead to more inflammation How does inflammation enhance phagocytosis?

    • This occurs through acute phase proteins:

    • The binding of these proteins to bacteria makes it easier for phagocytes to recognise the bacteria and engulf them

      • Mannose Binding Lectin (MBL): binds to bacteria that have mannose and fructose on their surface

      • C-reactive protein (CRP): binds to phosphocholine on bacterial surface

      • Complement proteins Results of the inflammatory response:

    • Leads to accumulation of fluid and cells at the site of infection

    • Redness, Heat, swelling, Pain, dysfunction of the affected area

      —

  2. Phagocytosis

    1. Neutrophils and macrophages ingest microbes and destroy them

    2. Its a process of ingesting particles larger than 0.5 0.5 μm in diameter

    3. Phagocytes generally patrol the body searching for pathogens

    4. They are able to respond to cytokines Steps of Phagocytosis:

      1. Chemotaxis: chemical signals that are released by damaged tissue or other immune cells guide the phagocyte towards the sites of infection

      2. Attachment: Once the phagocyte reaches the infected area, it uses receptors on its surface to attach to the foreign pathogen. These receptors recognise the antigens on the surface of the pathogen to ensure a proper match before engulfment

      3. Engulfment: The pathogen is engulfed and placed inside a vesicle called the phagosome

      4. The phagosome fuses with a lysozyme to form a phagolysosome

      5. Enzymes in the lysosome kill the pathogen and digest it/break it down

      6. Residual body formation: some indigestible particles remain, the phagolysosome is now called a residual body

      7. The residual body fuses with the phagocyte and discharges the waste material through exocytosis


        • When the phagocyte’s receptor bind to the pathogen, several enzymes are activated

        • One of these enzymes is called phagocyte oxidase and it rapidly convert oxygen into superoxide and free radicals.This process is called oxidative burst. These free radicals are toxic to the ingested pathogen.

        • A 2nd enzyme called Nitric oxide synthase converts arginine into Nitric oxide, which also kills pathogens

        • Lysosomal proteases breakdown microbial proteins

        • Inherited deficiency of the phagocyte oxidase enzyme is the cause of a disorder called chronic granulomatous disease (CGD)

    —

  3. Antiviral Defense

    • Viruses have a special type of response involving interferons, Natural killer cells and other

    • Type 1 IFNs (IFN-α and IFN-β) inhibit viral replication, which allows cells to become resistant to infection

    • This action is why IFN-α is used to treat some forms of chronic viral hepatitis.

    • Type 1 IFNs (IFN-α and IFN-β) are produced by dendritic cells and virus infected cells

    • They bind to receptors on both infected and uninfected cells

    • They activate pathways that activate enzymes that interfere with viral replication at different steps. They can do that by inhibiting viral protein translation or degrading viral RNA

    • Type 1 IFNs make it easier for CTLs (Cytotoxic T lymphocytes) to recognise the infected cell and kill it.
